Challenges with large ecological systems

  1. The possible number of combinations is very large as the pool of species grow

    • A pool of nn species can give rise to (2n1)(2n1) possible community assemblages (considering solely the presence and absence of species)

  2. Rarely will all assemblages lead to coexistence

    • Species might not coexist and larger communities collapse to smaller communities (triplets become co-cultures, co-cultures collapse to monocultures)

Fitting steps:

Our goal is to find a matrix BB such that our predictions for ~xx~ are as close as possible to our observed data.

Our proposed algorithm in inspired by an "expectation-maximization" algorithm

  1. Propose a candidate matrix BB
  2. Compute the corresponding matrix of endpoints XX and get predicted values
  3. "Score" the matrix by estimating SSQ and accept or reject new matrices according to its score

Connections to dynamical models

Fitting the statistical model is equivalent to finding a GLV model with an equilibrium structure that is as close as possible to the observed data.

In other words, we would be fitting the matrix BB encoding all the equilibria of the system

The model admits up to 2n2n equilibria, of which only one can have all positive components (called feasible coexistence equilibrium), and all the other are "boundary" equilibria in which a certain subset of species can coexist

Important note: using "static" observations we can only find the composite parameter Bij=aijriBij=aijri or B=D(r)1AB=D(r)1A and not AA and rr separately

Challenges and extensions

Relative abundances: Most microbial data come as relative abundances which would not work under this framework One of the limitations come from the fact that very distinct BB matrices would give rise to the same relative abundance distributions

Phylogeny: All species are ultimately connected and share an evolutionary history, which can be accounted for by studying their phylogenetic relationships. We can approximate BB by the variance-covariance matrix tht describes an ultrametric tree

Higher Order Interactions: at its heart, this method is a linear regression. We can also consider a modification of this model to include higher order interactions. This would amount to fitting (a much harder) polynomial regression of the form:

xki=ijiijxkjj,kiijlxkjxklxik=ijiijxjkj,kiijlxjkxlk

When will this method fail?

Lack of widespread coexistence: As we have seen, we need enough endpoints to fit BB, even when we rely on simplifications and approximations of BB

Strong multistability: for a given endpoint with species composition kk, the model assumes species would reach the same attractor. If that's not the case, the method would attempt to fit the average of the species' densities

Strong higher order interactions (HOI): this approach is based on pairwise interactions and cannot take into account strong non-linearities, HOI or context-dependency as when species ii and jj interact competitively but will interact mutualistically in the presence of a third species ll
